\section{Clause Splitting}
\label{task_1.1}
\subsection{Objective and Problem Understanding}
Legal contracts typically consist of long, complex sentences that include multiple clauses connected through conjunctions, conditions, and enumerations. These structures make downstream Natural Language Processing (NLP) tasks -- such as entity recognition or semantic role labeling -- significantly more difficult. Therefore, the goal of this task is to transform raw contract text into a sequence of semantically independent clauses, where each clause represents a meaningful and analyzable unit.

\medskip

To achieve this, the implementation focuses on designing a hybrid rule-based and dependency-aware clause segmentation algorithm that leverages both surface-level punctuation and syntactic structure.

\subsubsection*{Approach Overview:}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Rule-Based Splitting:}
        Initial clause candidates are extracted using heuristics based on surface features such as semicolons, periods, and conjunctions. These are strong signals of clause and sentence boundaries in legal English.
  \item \textbf{Dependency Parsing:}
        For challenging cases where punctuation is absent or ambiguous, a syntactic dependency parser identifies clausal structures in complex sentences.
  \item \textbf{Hybrid Postprocessing:}
        The outputs from both strategies are further refined. For example, enumerations and subordinate clauses attached to the main clause are separated to ensure semantic independence.
\end{itemize}
This combination of shallow and deep linguistic cues helps ensure that each segment produced is both syntactically and semantically meaningful for downstream tasks.

\subsection{Preprocessing of Legal Text}
Before clause splitting, the raw contract text is subjected to a dedicated preprocessing stage designed to clean and normalize the data. Legal documents, particularly those sourced from public filings, frequently contain artifacts such as redaction markers, extraneous page headers/footers, and inconsistent whitespace. If left unaddressed, these features can negatively impact the performance of subsequent NLP analyses -- especially clause segmentation.

\begin{minted}[fontsize=\footnotesize,breaklines,linenos]{python}
def clean_legal_text(text):
    """Removes redactions, page numbers, and formatting artifacts."""
    text = re.sub(r'\[\*\*\*\]', '', text)
    text = re.sub(r'Page \d+', '', text)
    text = re.sub(r'\n+', ' ', text)
    text = re.sub(r'\s+', ' ', text)
    return text.strip()
\end{minted}

The preprocessing function applies several key transformations:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Removal of redaction markers:} Textual placeholders such as \verb|[***]|, which indicate redacted or confidential content, are stripped from the document to prevent confusion with actual clause content.
  \item \textbf{Elimination of page numbering:} Regular expressions are used to identify and remove page headers or footers (e.g., ``Page 1'') that appear throughout scanned or OCR-processed contracts.
  \item \textbf{Whitespace normalization:} Multiple consecutive newline (\verb|\\n|) and space characters are replaced with a single space, ensuring that sentence and clause boundaries are not obscured by formatting noise.
\end{itemize}

Through these steps, the input text is transformed into a linguistically consistent form that is more amenable to parsing, tokenization, and clause boundary detection. The preprocessing routine helps maximize the precision and recall of the clause splitter by minimizing spurious tokens and misplaced boundaries in the downstream analysis.

To handle the unique complexities of legal texts, the algorithm is divided into three specialized heuristic rules.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{Dependency-Aware Clause Splitting}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{ExtractClauses}{$raw\_text$}
    \State $doc \gets$ \Call{PreprocessAndParse}{$raw\_text$}
    \State $clauses \gets [\ ]$, $start\_pos \gets 0$

    \For{\textbf{each} $token$ \textbf{in} $doc$}
    \State $split \gets \textbf{false}$

    \If{$token \in \{".", ";"\}$} \Comment{Rule 1: Hard Punctuation}
    \State $split \gets \textbf{true}$
    \ElsIf{$token = \text{":"}$ \textbf{and} \Call{Next}{$token$} = \text{"("}} \Comment{Rule 2: Look-Ahead}

    \EndIf

    \EndProcedure

\subsubsection*{Token Whitespace Preservation}
A critical challenge in NLP text segmentation is the \textbf{Tokenization Trap}. When parsers like \textcolor{red}{\textcolor{red}{\textcolor{red}{spaCy}}} process text, they split punctuation marks, symbols, and words into discrete tokens. Naively reconstructing a clause by joining these tokens with spaces ruins the original formatting, yielding artifacts such as \verb|( i )| instead of \verb|(i)| or ``V / MC Systems'' instead of ``V/MC Systems''.

\medskip

In the legal domain, preserving exact formatting is paramount. To resolve this, the algorithm abandons token-level string joining. Instead, it maintains index pointers (\verb|start_idx| and \verb|end_idx|) and slices the original document span (\verb|doc[start_idx:end_idx].text|). Because \textcolor{red}{\textcolor{red}{\textcolor{red}{spaCy}}} spans retain the exact character offsets of the raw input, this slicing technique guarantees 100\% fidelity to the original contract formatting, eliminating artificial whitespace while ensuring clean data for downstream tasks.
\subsubsection*{Handling Legal Enumerations}
Standard sentence boundary detection models are trained on general English, where periods indicate the end of a thought. However, legal contracts frequently utilize complex enumerations, where massive lists of conditions or obligations are chained together using semicolons (;). A standard tokenizer will fail on these structures, grouping multiple distinct conditions into a single, unmanageable paragraph.

\medskip

To address this, the algorithm explicitly defines the semicolon as a hard clause boundary. This isolates each condition into its own independent string.

\paragraph{Standard Parser Output (Failure):}
\begin{quote}
  \textit{``Such Reserve Account may be funded by: (i) one or more debits; (ii) one or more deductions; or (iii) ISO's delivery of a letter of credit.''}\\(Treated as one massive clause).
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Our Algorithm Output (Success):}
\begin{enumerate}[label=\textit{Clause \arabic*.}]
  \item Such Reserve Account may be funded by: (i) one or more debits;
  \item (ii) one or more deductions;
  \item or (iii) ISO's delivery of a letter of credit.
\end{enumerate}

By splitting explicitly at semicolons, the algorithm preserves the semantic independence of each list item. This is critical for Intent Classification task (\result{Section~\ref{task_2.3}}), as it allows the classifier to evaluate each condition individually rather than attempting to classify a convoluted block of text.

\subsubsection*{Syntactic Dependency Rules}
For complex sentences where punctuation is insufficient or ambiguous (such as clauses connected by ``and'', ``or'', or ``but''), the algorithm leverages dependency parsing to determine if a split is syntactically valid. Simply splitting at every coordinating conjunction (\verb|cc|) results in severe over-splitting, destroying compound adjectives or compound predicates (e.g., splitting ``Approval will not be withheld or delayed'' into two meaningless fragments).

\medskip

To prevent this, a strict \textbf{``New Subject'' Heuristic} was implemented. The algorithm will only split a sentence at a conjunction if \emph{all} of the following syntactic conditions are met:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item The token is a coordinating conjunction (\verb|dep_ == "cc"|).
  \item Its head is a verb (\verb|pos_ == "VERB"|).
  \item The head verb is connected to a secondary verb (\verb|dep_ == "conj"|).
  \item \textbf{Critical Check:} The secondary verb possesses its own explicit nominal subject (\verb|nsubj| or \verb|nsubjpass|).
\end{itemize}

Example of the heuristic in action:

\begin{quote}
  ``\textit{In the event a Merchant refuses to consent, ISO will notify SERVICERS, and Bank will have the right to terminate its sponsorship.}''
\end{quote}

The parser detects the conjunction ``and'', identifies ``will notify'' and ``will have'' as the two connected verbs, and verifies that ``Bank'' is the explicit subject of the second verb. Because a new subject is present, it is a truly independent clause, and the algorithm safely cleanly severs the string before the conjunction.

\section{Noun Phrase Chunking}
\label{task_1.2}
Following the isolation of semantically independent clauses in \result{Section~\ref{task_1.1}}, the next critical phase in the NLP extraction pipeline is \textbf{Noun Phrase (NP) Chunking}. The primary objective of this task is to identify and extract non-recursive noun phrases from the segmented text, representing their boundaries using the standard \textbf{Inside-Outside-Beginning (IOB)} tagging schema.

\subsection{The Linguistic Challenge in Legal Text}
In general domain text, entities are often single words or simple bigrams (e.g., ``the company''). However, \textbf{commercial contracts exhibit extreme technical density}, making entity extraction significantly more complex. As highlighted in the dataset overview, documents such as the Distributor Agreement frequently contain entities realized as complex, multi-word syntactic constituents -- for example, ``G3001 GPSLink mobile terminal'' or ``MidLink Middleware software.''

If an NLP parser fails to recognize these multi-word strings as a single noun phrase, it will fracture the entity. For example, a naive parser might tag ``mobile'' as an adjective and ``terminal'' as a noun, losing the fact that the entire four-word string represents a single, indivisible hardware product governed by the contract.

\subsubsection*{Strategic Importance to the Pipeline}
Noun Phrase Chunking is not merely a formatting exercise; it is a foundational prerequisite for the advanced machine learning objectives in \result{Assignment~\ref{assignment_2}}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Semantic Boundary Definition} (\result{Section~\ref{task_2.1}}): By grouping determiners, adjectives, and nouns into a single chunk, the algorithm establishes the exact token boundaries required to train a \textit{Custom Named Entity Recognition} model on intricate entities (e.g., complex product names, precise numerical and temporal phrases).
  \item \textbf{Contextualizing Obligations} (\result{Section~\ref{task_2.3}}): While Clause Splitting (Task 1.1) isolates what the contractual rule is (the predicate), Noun Phrase Chunking isolates the \textit{actors} and \textit{objects} involved. It identifies who is obligated (e.g., ``SERVICERS'') and what specific object or payment is being acted upon (e.g., ``Net Program Participation Fees'').
\end{itemize}
By accurately mapping these phrases into the \texttt{Token~\textbackslash t~Tag} tabular format required by the problem specification, the pipeline effectively transforms unstructured linguistic text into discrete, machine-readable objects, ready for entity classification and downstream retrieval.
\subsection{Methodology: The IOB Tagging Schema}
The objective of this module is to identify key entities -- such as contracting parties, financial amounts, and legal deadlines -- by labeling noun phrases (NPs) within extracted clauses. To structure the extracted clauses for downstream entity recognition, the pipeline implements the \textbf{Inside-Outside-Beginning (IOB)} tagging schema. This standard sequence-labeling format allows the system to represent the precise boundaries of multi-word noun phrases (NPs) at the token level.
\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{IOB Noun Phrase Chunking with Boundary Reset}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{GenerateIOBTags}{clause}
    \State $doc \gets$ \Call{NLP\_Parse}{clause}
    \State $tags \gets$ \text{array of "O" with length equal to number of tokens in $doc$}
    \For{\textbf{each} $chunk$ \textbf{in} $doc$.noun\_chunks}
    % main for loop begins here
    \State $start\_new \gets \textbf{true}$
    \For{\textbf{each} $token$ \textbf{in} $chunk$}
    \If{\Call{IsExcluded}{$token$}} \Comment{Filter punct, preps, etc.}
    \State $tags[token] \gets \text{"O"}$
    \State $start\_new \gets \textbf{true}$ \Comment{Reset NP boundary}
    \Else
    \State $tags[token] \gets start\_new \ ? \ \text{"B-NP"} : \text{"I-NP"}$
    \State $start\_new \gets \textbf{false}$
    \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \EndFor

    \State \Return \Call{FormatOutput}{$doc, tags$}
    \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}
\paragraph{Dependency-Based Chunking}
Instead of using a simple Regular Expression (RegEx) parser, we implemented a methodology leveraging spaCy’s dependency parser. This allows the system to:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Handle Complex Legal Syntax:} Legal text often contains nested modifiers. By utilizing the \texttt{noun\_chunks} iterator, we identify phrases based on their syntactic ``head'' noun rather than just linear word order.
  \item \textbf{IOB Tagging Scheme Implementation:} We map the detected chunks to the required IOB format:
        \begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
          \item \textbf{B-NP (Beginning of Noun Phrase):} This tag is exclusively assigned to the very first token of a recognized noun phrase. It signals to the downstream model that a new entity or object boundary has started.
          \item \textbf{I-NP (Inside a Noun Phrase):} This tag is assigned to all subsequent tokens that belong to the same noun phrase initiated by the B-NP tag.
          \item \textbf{O (Outside):} This tag is assigned to any token that does not belong to a noun phrase. In legal contracts, this typically includes verbs, prepositions, coordinating conjunctions, and hard punctuation.
        \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Refinement and Edge Case Handling}
To meet the specific requirements of legal document processing, the following logic adjustments were made:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Punctuation Filtering:} Standard legal punctuation (e.g., commas, periods, hyphens) is forced to the O tag to maintain clean boundaries between distinct legal entities.
  \item \textbf{Strict Boundary Resets:} We implemented a ``reset'' mechanism where any token following an O tag must start with B-NP if it belongs to a new noun phrase. This prevents ``broken'' sequences where an I-NP incorrectly follows an O tag.
  \item \textbf{Financial and Mathematical Symbols:} Special handling was applied to currency symbols (e.g., \textdollar) and operators (e.g., =) to ensure financial calculations and amounts remain semantically intact within their respective chunks.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Qualitative Evaluation}
The effectiveness of the dependency-aware Noun Phrase Chunking module is evaluated by examining its ability to maintain syntactic continuity across complex legal ownership structures. Traditional shallow parsers frequently fracture multi-word entities when encountering punctuation, coordinating conjunctions, or possessive markers. To demonstrate the robustness of the implemented IOB tagging schema, the following sample from the \textit{Sponsorship Agreement} illustrates the algorithm's handling of chained possessives and coordinated actors:

  \begin{tabular}{l|llllllll}
    \textbf{Token}   & Merchant & 's   & or   & ISO  & 's   & participation & in & MasterCard \\
    \hline
    \textbf{IOB Tag} & B-NP     & I-NP & I-NP & I-NP & I-NP & I-NP          & O  & B-NP       \\

As evidenced by the output above, the parser successfully maps the entire phrase \texttt{Merchant's or ISO's participation} as a single, contiguous semantic unit.

\begin{itemize}[topsep=1pt, itemsep=0pt, parsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Possessive Integrity:} The algorithm correctly identifies that the possessive suffixes (\texttt{'s}) are not independent tokens to be excluded, but rather integral modifiers linking the contracting parties (\texttt{Merchant}, \texttt{ISO}) to the target object (\texttt{participation}). Furthermore, the implementation guarantees strict sequence adherence through its boundary reset logic. \medskip

By dynamically resetting the beginning tag after any excluded token, the algorithm ensures that every newly initiated noun phrase strictly begins with a B-NP. The transition from \texttt{participation} (I-NP) to \texttt{in} (O) and immediately to \texttt{MasterCard} (B-NP) proves that the extraction sequence is fully sanitized, resolving the broken chunk vulnerability and generating an artifact that is perfectly aligned with standard sequence-labeling requirements.

\section{Dependency Analysis}
\label{task_1.3}
\subsection{Objective and Syntactic Mapping}
The final component of the Assignment 1 NLP pipeline is \textbf{Dependency Analysis}. While Clause Splitting (\result{Section~\ref{task_1.1}}) isolated the semantic boundaries of the text and Noun Phrase Chunking (\result{Section~\ref{task_1.2}}) identified the specific entities involved, the objective of Dependency Analysis is to uncover the grammatical skeleton connecting them. This task extracts and maps the word-to-word \textit{head-dependent} relationships for every token within a given clause, creating a structured tree of syntactic dependencies.

\subsubsection*{The Linguistic Utility in Legal English}
Commercial contracts are notoriously dense, frequently employing passive voice, deep embedding, and vast distances between a governing verb and its associated subjects or objects. For instance, in a complex termination clause, a shallow keyword search or simple Part-of-Speech (POS) tagger might struggle to map the exact relationship between the acting party and the action due to the sheer volume of surrounding legal modifiers and conditions.

\medskip

Dependency mapping resolves this ambiguity by translating the linear text into a directed graph of the sentence syntax. By identifying the root predicate (\texttt{ROOT} = ``terminate'') and tracing its directed edges to the nominal subject (\texttt{nsubj} = ``ISO'') and the direct object (\texttt{dobj} = ``Agreement''), the pipeline exposes the core action of the clause regardless of word distance, intervening noise, or convoluted phrasing.

\medskip

This token-level syntactic mapping is not only a formal requirement of the project but also a critical necessity for advanced relation extraction. It provides the exact structural logic required to populate the target JSON schema, enabling downstream machine learning models to programmatically understand who is doing what to whom within the contract.

\subsection{Methodology: The Universal Dependencies Framework}
To programmatically extract the grammatical relationships within the segmented clauses, the pipeline utilizes the \textbf{Universal Dependencies (UD)} framework. UD is a standardized, cross-linguistic annotation schema that categorizes syntactic relationships between a ``head'' token and its syntactic ``dependents.''
\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{Syntactic Dependency Extraction and JSON Serialization}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{GenerateDependencyJSON}{$clauses$}
    \State $dataset\_array \gets [\ ]$
    \For{\textbf{each} $clause$ \textbf{in} $clauses$}
    \State $dependencies \gets$ \Call{ExtractDependencies}{$clause$}
    \State $clause\_record \gets \{\text{"Clause"}{:}\ clause,\ \text{"Dependencies"}{:}\ dependencies\}$
    \State \Call{Append}{$dataset\_array,\ clause\_record$}
    \EndFor
    \State \Return $dataset\_array$
    \EndProcedure
    \Procedure{ExtractDependencies}{$clause$}
    \State $doc \gets$ \Call{DependencyParse}{$clause$} \Comment{Generate directed syntactic graph}
    \State $token\_mappings \gets [\ ]$
    \For{\textbf{each} $token$ \textbf{in} $doc$}
    \If{\texttt{token.text.strip() $\neq$ ""}} \Comment{Skip whitespace and empty tokens}
    \State $dep\_entry \gets \{$
    \State \quad $\text{"Token"}{:}\ token.text,$
    \State \quad $\text{"Head"}{:}\ token.head.text,$ \Comment{String form of syntactic governor}
    \State \quad $\text{"Dependency"}{:}\ token.dep\_label$
    \State $\}$
    \State \Call{Append}{$token\_mappings,\ dep\_entry$}
    \EndIf
    \EndFor
    \State \Return $token\_mappings$
    \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsubsection*{The Parsing Engine}
The implementation leverages \textcolor{red}{spaCy}'s \texttt{en\_core\_web\_sm} model, which features a highly optimized, transition-based dependency parser. Unlike traditional constituency parsing -- which breaks sentences into nested phrase brackets -- transition-based dependency parsing establishes direct, directed arcs between individual words. During execution, the parser analyzes the topological structure of the clause and assigns a specific syntactic dependency label to every token. Key labels crucial for legal text analysis include:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{ROOT}: The primary predicate or main verb of the clause (e.g., ``terminate'', ``warrant'', ``pay''). All other words in the sentence ultimately trace their dependency back to this root.
  \item \textbf{nsubj} / \textbf{nsubjpass}: The nominal subject (or passive subject) performing the action, which usually identifies the obligated or acting party (e.g., ``ISO'', ``SERVICERS'').
  \item \textbf{dobj} / \textbf{pobj}: The direct object of a verb or the object of a preposition, typically identifying the target of an action or rule (e.g., ``Agreement'', ``fees'').
  \item \textbf{amod}: Adjectival modifiers that define the exact scope of legal entities (e.g., ``prior'' and ``written'' modifying ``notice'').
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Handling Legal Modifiers and Complexity}
Commercial contracts are characterized by extreme syntactic depth, heavily relying on chained prepositional phrases and clausal complements (e.g., ``in accordance with the terms of this Agreement'').

\medskip

The dependency-based methodology excels in this environment because it systematically decomposes these chains without losing the governing logic. The parser links prepositions (\texttt{prep}) to their governing verbs or nouns, and then maps them to their subsequent prepositional objects (\texttt{pobj}). By mapping these relationships as a directed graph, the pipeline ensures that subordinate conditions and complex legal modifiers do not break the analytical chain. This allows the system to trace a complete syntactic pathway from a deep modifier all the way back to the governing \texttt{ROOT} verb, capturing the full structural reality of the clause.
\subsection{Data Representation and JSON Schema}
To transform the directed acyclic graph (DAG) generated by the dependency parser into a serializable format, the system implements a mapping function that iterates through the parsed text. Each token is converted into a structured entry within a JSON array, ensuring that the syntactic hierarchy is preserved through explicitly defined \texttt{Head} pointers.

\subsubsection*{The Mapping Logic}
\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Serialization:} The implementation captures the literal string of each token (\texttt{Token}) and its immediate syntactic governor (\texttt{Head}). By representing the head as its string form (not as a numeric index), the structure remains human-readable and easily verifiable.
  \item \textbf{Labeling:} The \texttt{Dependency} field utilizes standardized Universal Dependencies labels (such as \texttt{nsubj}, \texttt{ROOT}), explicitly codifying the grammatical relation for each token.
  \item \textbf{Noise Reduction:} Before converting to JSON, the extraction process skips over any non-semantic tokens (such as whitespace or stray newlines) using a simple filter (\texttt{if token.text.strip():}). This prevents OCR or formatting artifacts from polluting the output.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{Output Structure}
The final output aggregates this information into a nested JSON array, where each entry contains a segmented clause and a list of its token-level dependency mappings:\clearpage

  "Dependencies": [
    { "Token": "ISO",       "Head": "terminate", "Dependency": "nsubj" },
    { "Token": "may",       "Head": "terminate", "Dependency": "aux"   },
    { "Token": "terminate", "Head": "terminate", "Dependency": "ROOT"  },
    { "Token": "this",      "Head": "Agreement", "Dependency": "det"   },

  ]
}
\end{minted}

This dictionary-based representation fulfills the data formatting requirements and provides a clean, machine-readable structure that supports both human audit and downstream automation for retrieval or relation extraction.

\section{Custom Named Entity Recognition}
\label{task_2.1}
General-purpose Named Entity Recognition (NER) systems are highly effective for standard natural language but consistently underperform when applied to the dense, idiosyncratic domain of legal contracts. Standard NER models frequently fragment long corporate names, fail to distinguish between generic organizations and legally bound parties, and lack the domain-specific vocabulary to identify critical structural references (e.g., internal sections, exhibits, or governing policies). In complex documents, this lack of syntactic and semantic precision prevents the accurate extraction of ``who does what,'' rendering the data unusable for downstream automated reasoning or Question Answering (QA) tasks.
\subsubsection*{Objective}
The primary objective of this task is to develop a domain-specific NER pipeline by fine-tuning a pre-trained language model (\verb|en\_core\_web\_md| via spaCy). This pipeline transforms unstructured legal clauses into structured, machine-readable representations by accurately identifying and extracting contract-specific entities.

\subsection{Custom Schema Engineering and Justification}
The project specification notes that the entity schema ``may include (but is not limited to)'' financial and penal variables such as \texttt{MONEY}, \texttt{RATE}, and \texttt{PENALTY}. However, an exploratory analysis of the specific corpus selected for this task -- a complex Sponsorship Agreement involving multiple corporate entities -- revealed that financial variables were too sparse to generate a robust training signal. Attempting to force the model to learn these sparse variables resulted in severe hallucinations and misclassification of standard text.

\medskip

Therefore, to maximize extraction precision and align with the actual semantic reality of the selected contract, a highly tailored custom schema was engineered. The document's core logic relies heavily on policy adherence, structural cross-referencing, and party obligations rather than transactional financial penalties. The final schema is defined as follows:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=3.5cm, style=nextline]
  \item[PARTY:] Any legally defined company, organization, or role. These are strictly modeled as continuous spans to prevent entity fragmentation.
  \item[AGREEMENT:] Explicit references to governing documents.
  \item[SECTION:] Internal structural references necessary for tracing contractual logic.
  \item[EXHIBIT:] External structural references, attachments, and schedules.
  \item[DATE:] Timeframes, deadlines, and specific temporal milestones, including relative legal dates.
  \item[POLICY:] Governing rules, standards, and guidelines that dictate party behavior and compliance.
\end{description}

\medskip

By aligning the NER schema directly with the architectural reality of the specific contract, this pipeline prioritizes high-precision extraction of structural and relational entities, establishing a reliable data foundation for subsequent Semantic Role Labeling and intent classification.

\subsection{Training Dataset Generation \& Preprocessing}
\subsubsection{Data Sourcing and Annotation Strategy}
The training dataset was constructed by manually extracting and annotating complex clauses from the core Sponsorship Agreement. To ensure the model learned precise entity boundaries without being confused by domain-specific noise, strict annotation guidelines were enforced:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Atomic Entity Spans:} Corporate entities and legal names were annotated as single, continuous spans -- for example, separating ``Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.'' from ``First Data Merchant Services Corporation'' rather than grouping them.
  \item \textbf{Exclusion of Variable Noise:} Highly irregular contact information (such as physical addresses, zip codes, and facsimile numbers) were deliberately tagged as O (\textit{Outside}) to prevent the model from misclassifying them as \texttt{SECTION} or \texttt{PARTY} entities.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Data Validation and Format Conversion}

Standard deep learning pipelines require data to be rigorously formatted and token-aligned. The raw JSON dataset was preprocessed and converted into spaCy's highly optimized binary format (\texttt{DocBin}) using a custom Python script. During this preprocessing phase, two critical validation steps were executed:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Schema Enforcement:} The script systematically filtered the incoming annotations against the strict target schema (\texttt{["PARTY", "AGREEMENT", "SECTION", "EXHIBIT", "DATE", "POLICY"]}).   \item \textbf{Token Alignment Validation:} The \texttt{alignment\_mode="expand"} parameter was utilized during span creation. If a human annotator accidentally selected half a word (e.g., missing a trailing character), the preprocessor automatically expanded the boundary to encompass the nearest valid linguistic token, ensuring the neural network received mathematically clean inputs. Misaligned spans that could not be resolved were logged and skipped.
\end{itemize}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{Legal Entity Preprocessing and Binary Serialization}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{ConvertJSONToDocBin}{$\mathcal{D}_{json}, \mathcal{L}$}
    \State $DocBin \gets$ \Call{InitializeDocBin}{}

\subsubsection*{Mitigating Catastrophic Forgetting (Weight Preservation)}
During the initial implementation iterations, a critical architectural flaw was identified: initializing the model via standard training loops effectively randomized the underlying network weights, destroying the model's pre-existing knowledge of English syntax. This resulted in severe classification anomalies, such as predicting standard nouns as legal dates.

\medskip

To resolve this, the fine-tuning strategy was heavily modified. Instead of initializing a new model, the pipeline utilizes a \texttt{resume\_training()} protocol. This strategy locks the foundational language representations and only updates the weights of the NER output layer. By doing so, the model retains its baseline capacity to parse English grammar while simultaneously learning the new mathematical boundaries of the domain-specific schema (\texttt{PARTY}, \texttt{AGREEMENT}, etc.).

\subsubsection*{Pipeline Component Freezing}
A spaCy processing pipeline consists of multiple sequential components (e.g., \texttt{tok2vec}, \texttt{tagger}, \texttt{parser}, \texttt{attribute\_ruler}, \texttt{lemmatizer}). Because this task strictly targets Named Entity Recognition, backpropagating loss through the entire pipeline introduces unnecessary computational overhead and risks corrupting the syntactical parsing layers (which are required for the Dependency Analysis task in \result{Section~\ref{task_1.3}}).

\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{Domain-Specific NER Fine-Tuning and Optimization}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{FineTuneNERModel}{$\mathcal{D}_{raw}, \mathcal{L}, E$}
    \State $\mathcal{M} \leftarrow$ \Call{Load}{"en\_core\_web\_md"} \Comment{Load pre-trained model with word vectors}
    \State $ner \leftarrow \mathcal{M}.\text{get\_pipe}(\text{"ner"})$
    \For{\textbf{each} $label$ \textbf{in} $\mathcal{L}$} \Comment{Apply custom schema}
    \State \Call{AddLabel}{$ner, label$}
    \EndFor
    \State $\mathcal{D}_{bin} \leftarrow$ \Call{PreprocessAndAlign}{$\mathcal{D}_{raw}, \mathcal{L}$} \Comment{Binary conversion and span alignment}
    \State $Opt \leftarrow \mathcal{M}.\text{resume\_training}()$ \Comment{Preserve base language weights}
    \State \Call{FreezePipes}{$\mathcal{M}, \text{except}=\text{"ner"}$} \Comment{Isolate gradients to NER head}
    \For{$epoch \gets 1$ \textbf{to} $E$}
    \State $\mathcal{D}_{shuffled} \gets$ \Call{Shuffle}{$\mathcal{D}_{bin}$}
    \State $Batches \gets$ \Call{CreateCompoundingBatches}{$\mathcal{D}_{shuffled}, \text{start}=4, \text{end}=32$}
    \For{\textbf{each} $batch$ \textbf{in} $Batches$}
    \State $\mathcal{M}.\text{update}(batch, \text{optimizer}=Opt, \text{dropout}=0.3)$
    \EndFor
    \EndFor
    \State \Call{SaveToDisk}{$\mathcal{M}, \text{"ner\_model"}$}
    \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

To isolate the training signal, all pipeline components other than the \texttt{ner} component were explicitly frozen (disabled) prior to the training loop. This ensures that gradient updates are applied exclusively to the entity recognition head.

\subsubsection*{Training Dynamics and Hyperparameter Optimization}
To ensure stable convergence and prevent overfitting on the small dataset, the training loop was optimized with the following hyperparameters and techniques:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Dynamic Minibatching:} Updating weights after every single example (batch size of 1) produces highly erratic gradient trajectories. To stabilize learning, training examples were grouped using a compounding mini-batch strategy. The batch size starts at 4.0 and exponentially increases to 32.0 (using a 1.001 compounding rate), allowing the model to make granular adjustments early in training and broader generalizations in later epochs.
  \item \textbf{Dropout Regularization:} A dropout rate of 0.3 (30\%) was applied during the updates. By randomly dropping network connections during training, the model is forced to learn redundant representations of the entities, preventing it from simply memorizing the training text.
  \item \textbf{Epochs:} The model achieved optimal fine-tuning convergence within 30 to 40 epochs. Training beyond this threshold yielded diminishing returns on loss reduction and increased the risk of overfitting the specific corporate names present in the training set.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Technical Limitations and Trade-offs}
\subsubsection{Domain-Specific Noise and the ``Address'' Limitation}

Legal contracts often embed this information within densely packed ``Notices'' clauses. During the early iterations of model training, an attempt was made to force the NER model to categorize these strings. However, due to the extreme structural variance of addresses and the limited size of the custom annotated dataset, the model suffered from severe categorical hallucinations, attempting to force street names and zip codes into the existing schema.

\subsubsection{The Trade-off: Precision over Semantic Scope}

To resolve this failure, a deliberate engineering trade-off was made: \textit{Precision was prioritized over Semantic Scope}. Rather than polluting the target schema with highly irregular \texttt{ADDRESS} or \texttt{FAX} labels -- which would require thousands of annotated examples to stabilize -- these entities were explicitly excluded from the training data (tagged as O / Outside). By enforcing strict span boundaries that stop immediately after the corporate name, the model was prevented from ``guessing'' labels for the subsequent contact noise.

\medskip

This trade-off means the current pipeline cannot extract routing or geographical data. However, this limitation is deemed acceptable because extracting the core legal actors (\texttt{PARTY}) and governing rules (\texttt{POLICY}, \texttt{DATE}) is vastly more critical for the downstream Semantic Role Labeling task than identifying a physical mailing address.

\subsubsection{Future Work and Mitigation Strategy}

Relying solely on a neural network to extract deterministic data (like zip codes or fax numbers) is computationally inefficient and prone to error. In future iterations, this limitation can be mitigated by transitioning from a monolithic NER model to a \textbf{Hybrid Extraction Pipeline}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Semantic Layer:} The fine-tuned Transformer model handles context-dependent legal variables (\texttt{PARTY}, \texttt{AGREEMENT}, \texttt{POLICY}).
  \item \textbf{Deterministic Layer:} A secondary module utilizing Regular Expressions (Regex) processes the text post-inference to confidently extract highly patterned data, such as \verb|\d{3}-\d{3}-\d{4}| for facsimile numbers.
\end{itemize}

\section{Semantic Role Labeling}
\label{task_2.2}

While Named Entity Recognition (NER) successfully isolates static legal variables (e.g., \texttt{PARTY}, \texttt{DATE}, \texttt{POLICY}), it inherently lacks the structural awareness to interpret how these entities interact. The primary objective of the Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) task is to transition from discrete entity extraction to \textbf{relational mapping}. In the context of legal contracts, this means moving beyond merely identifying that a Party and a Date exist in a clause, to \emph{programmatically extracting the underlying obligations} -- specifically, clarifying \textbf{who} is required to perform an action, \textbf{what} that action is, and \textbf{when} it must be completed. This relational mapping forms the deterministic foundation required for downstream automated reasoning and query resolution.

\subsubsection*{Specification Alignment}
To satisfy the strict academic specifications of this task, the extraction pipeline must accurately assign semantic roles to the entities identified in the previous stage. The system is required to anchor these roles relative to the main predicate (the core verb or action) of each individual clause. The implementation maps the unstructured text to the following six required roles:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.4cm, style=nextline]
  \item[Predicate:] The primary action, duty, or requirement governing the clause.
  \item[Agent:] The acting entity (typically a \texttt{PARTY}) obligated to perform the predicate.
  \item[Theme:] The object, concept, or document being acted upon.
  \item[Recipient:] The specific beneficiary or receiver of the action, payment, or notice.
  \item[Time:] The temporal boundaries, deadlines, or durations associated with the obligation.
  \item[Condition:] The specific prerequisites, exceptions, or logical states under which the primary obligation is triggered or voided.
\end{description}

By strictly mapping the text to these six dimensions, the pipeline successfully transforms highly variable legal phrasing into a standardized, machine-readable format ready for vectorization.

\subsection{Methodology: The QA-SRL Paradigm}
Traditional Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) systems often approach extraction as a sequence-labeling or token-classification problem, assigning BIO (Begin, Inside, Outside) tags to tokens that are then mapped to generic PropBank arguments (e.g., ARG0, ARG1). While effective for short, well-bounded sentences, this approach struggles with the dense and highly structured nature of legal contract language. Legal clauses frequently comprise compound sentences, multiple interacting verbs, embedded definitions, and long-range dependencies -- for example: \emph{``If Party A delivers the software, Party B shall pay the fee within 30 days''}. In such scenarios, a simple token classifier may fail to correctly anchor each argument to the appropriate predicate, or may miss relationships that are not explicitly adjacent in the text.

\subsubsection{Architectural Justification}
To overcome these limitations, our pipeline deliberately reframes Semantic Role Labeling as an \textbf{Extractive Question Answering (QA-SRL)} task. Rather than tagging tokens, the system employs a Machine Reading Comprehension (MRC) model -- specifically, a Transformer-based architecture -- that is prompted with clause-level, natural language questions. For each required semantic role (e.g., Predicate, Agent, Theme, Recipient, Time, Condition), the model is queried using a tailored, explicit question (e.g., ``Who is required to perform the action?''). The model's bidirectional attention enables evaluation of the \emph{entire clause context}, not just nearby tokens, thus allowing it to reliably extract arguments even when they are separated by intervening phrases, nested conditions, or legal boilerplate.

This QA-SRL approach provides several architectural advantages:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Context Awareness:} The Transformer model assesses the clause holistically, improving accuracy in extracting roles from complex or multi-predicate sentences.
  \item \textbf{Natural Language Alignment:} By framing extraction as question answering, the system is inherently explainable and transparent -- each extracted role is directly linked to a question interpretable by legal domain experts.
  \item \textbf{Extensibility:} The approach easily accommodates the addition of new role queries or contract-specific role types, increasing flexibility compared to fixed tag sets.
  \item \textbf{Reduced Fragility:} By abandoning token-level heuristics, the pipeline is more robust to varied clause structures and legal drafting idiosyncrasies.
\end{itemize}

Ultimately, the shift from token-based tagging to extractive QA forms the backbone of a semantic pipeline capable of handling the inherent complexity and ambiguity of real-world legal contracts.

\subsubsection{Model Selection}
To implement the QA-SRL architecture, the system utilizes the \texttt{deepset/minilm-uncased-squad2} model. This model is a highly optimized MiniLM-BERT Transformer that has been explicitly fine-tuned on the Stanford Question Answering Dataset (SQuAD~2.0).
\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.9\textwidth]{images/deepset_minilm-uncased-squad2.png}
  \label{deepset_minilm-uncased-squad2}
\end{figure}
This specific architecture was selected for three primary reasons:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Extractive Precision:} It is designed to extract exact text spans from a given context, aligning perfectly with the requirement to pull exact legal phrasing rather than generating abstract summaries.

  \item \textbf{Confidence Scoring:} The SQuAD~2.0 fine-tuning enables the model to output a null prediction (or a very low confidence score) when an answer does \emph{not} exist in the text. This is critical for legal parsing, as it prevents the model from hallucinating a \texttt{Condition} or \texttt{Time} constraint in a clause where none is explicitly stated.

  \item \textbf{Computational Efficiency:} As a distilled model ($\sim$130MB), it executes inference rapidly, making it viable for processing hundreds of clauses in a production pipeline without the memory overhead of larger monolithic LLMs.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Implementation and Prompt Engineering}

\subsubsection{Interrogative Schema Mapping}

To implement the Extractive Question-Answering pipeline, the academic semantic roles specified in the assignment rubric were translated into precise, natural language interrogations. The design of these prompts is critical; they must be broad enough to capture the variations in legal drafting, yet specific enough to prevent the model from returning irrelevant spans.

\medskip

The \texttt{deepset/minilm-uncased-squad2} model was tasked with evaluating every clause against the following prompt schema:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{Predicate:} \emph{``What is the main action, requirement, or verb?''}\\
        (Extracts the core operational mandate, e.g., ``shall terminate'' or ``agrees to pay''.)
  \item \textbf{Agent:} \emph{``Who is required to perform the action?''}\\
        (Identifies the primary actor or obligated party.)
  \item \textbf{Theme:} \emph{``What is the object, topic, or document being acted upon?''}\\
        (Captures the focus of the predicate, such as ``the Software'' or ``written notice''.)
  \item \textbf{Recipient:} \emph{``Who receives the payment, notice, or benefit?''}\\
        (Isolates the beneficiary or secondary party.)
  \item \textbf{Time:} \emph{``When does this happen or what is the duration?''}\\
        (Extracts temporal modifiers and deadlines.)
  \item \textbf{Condition:} \emph{``Under what condition, exception, or requirement does this apply?''}\\
        (Identifies the logical triggers or prerequisites for the action.)
\end{itemize}

By looping this schema over the extracted clauses, the pipeline systematically forces the BERT architecture to resolve the semantic roles based on deep bidirectional context.

\subsubsection{Confidence Thresholding and Hallucination Mitigation}
A major challenge in querying generative or extractive models is the tendency to ``hallucinate'' or force an answer even when the target variable does not exist. In legal contracts, many clauses are declarative and do not contain conditional logic or temporal constraints. Forcing a model to extract a Time role from a clause that lacks one compromises the integrity of the database.

\medskip

To mitigate this, the implementation leverages the inherent confidence scoring of the SQuAD~2.0-trained model. The model calculates a probability score for each extracted span. A strict threshold was engineered into the pipeline (\texttt{score > 0.05}). If the model's confidence in an answer falls below this threshold, the role is classified as \texttt{<Null>} and omitted from the JSON output. This deterministic guardrail ensures that the resulting database remains highly precise, prioritizing the exclusion of false positives over forcing incomplete semantic maps.

\subsubsection{Algorithmic Execution and Output Schema}

\paragraph{The Extraction Algorithm:}
The pipeline operationalizes the prompt schema and confidence thresholds through a deterministic iteration loop. For every semantically independent clause extracted during the syntax phase, the algorithm sequentially injects the clause and the interrogative prompts into the MiniLM-BERT model. To ensure the integrity of the downstream database, the algorithm mandates that a Predicate must be confidently identified before a clause is saved; clauses yielding only disconnected arguments are discarded as incomplete or unparseable.
\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{QA-SRL: Clause-Level Semantic Role Extraction}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{ExtractSemanticRoles}{$C, Q$} \Comment{$C$: Input Clauses, $Q$: Interrogative Prompts}
    \State $Model \gets$ \Call{Load}{``minilm-uncased-squad2''}
    \State $ExtractedData \gets [\ ]$

    \For{\textbf{each} $clause$ \textbf{in} $C$}
    \If{\Call{WordCount}{$clause$} $< 4$}
    \State \textbf{continue} \Comment{Discard non-semantic text fragments}
    \EndIf

    \State $roles \gets \{\}$

    \For{\textbf{each} $(role, prompt)$ \textbf{in} $Q$}
    \State $answer, conf\_score \gets Model.\text{predict}(prompt, clause)$

    \If{$conf\_score > 0.05$} \Comment{Filter out low-confidence hallucinations}
    \State $roles[role] \gets answer$
    \EndIf
    \EndFor

    \If{`Predicate' \textbf{in} $roles$} \Comment{Ensure the clause contains a valid main action}
    \State $record \gets$ \{ \texttt{``clause''}: $clause$, \texttt{``roles''}: $roles$ \}
    \State \Call{Append}{$ExtractedData$, $record$}
    \EndIf
    \EndFor

    \State \Return \Call{SerializeToJSON}{$ExtractedData$}
    \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Output Data Structure (JSON):}
To serve as the relational database for the \result{Assignment~\ref{assignment_3}} RAG Chatbot, the extracted variables are serialized into a highly structured JSON format (\texttt{srl\_results.json}). Each valid clause is mapped to an object containing four critical key-value pairs:

\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \texttt{clause}: The raw text string of the segmented legal provision.
  \item \texttt{predicate}: The core action, verb, or requirement successfully isolated by the QA model.
  \item \texttt{roles}: A nested dictionary containing the successfully extracted semantic arguments (e.g., Agent, Theme, Time, Condition, Recipient). Roles that fell below the confidence threshold are omitted to maintain strict data precision.
  \item \texttt{entities\_present}: A list of static entities (such as specific corporate names or document titles) previously identified by the NER module in \result{Section~\ref{task_2.1}}, explicitly linking the semantic roles to the static actors.
\end{itemize}

This nested representation effectively translates unstructured legal prose into a queryable, deterministic matrix, cleanly concluding the Information Extraction phase.

\subsection{Limitations and Error Analysis}
While framing Semantic Role Labeling (SRL) as an Extractive Question-Answering (QA) task successfully resolved the tokenization and framework limitations discussed in \result{Section~\ref{task_2.2}}, the approach is not without flaws. A rigorous audit of the \texttt{srl\_results.json} output reveals that the \texttt{minilm-uncased-squad2} model introduces new vulnerabilities specific to Machine Reading Comprehension (MRC), particularly when processing densely nested legal syntax and boilerplate enumerations.

\subsubsection*{Semantic Overwriting and ``Span Bleed'' in Multi-Predicate Sentences}

The most severe limitation of the QA-SRL paradigm occurs in highly complex, multi-predicate sentences. Because the model evaluates each role question independently, it can become statistically ``distracted'' by strong secondary clauses, completely missing the primary governing obligation. This results in \emph{span bleed}, where the model incorrectly assigns the same text span to multiple, contradictory roles.

\medskip

\begin{quote}
  \texttt{``ISO shall cease its use of any Application Materials that become unacceptable to SERVICERS within ninety (90) days of receipt of written notice from SERVICERS or such shorter period of time as may be required to comply with the Rules or to prevent a loss to SERVICERS.''}
\end{quote}

\begin{minted}[fontsize=\footnotesize,breaklines,linenos]{json}
{
  "predicate": "to comply with the Rules or to prevent a loss to SERVICERS",
  "roles": {
    "Agent": "to comply with the Rules or to prevent a loss to SERVICERS",
    "Theme": "Application Materials",
    "Recipient": "SERVICERS",
    "Time": "ninety (90) days",
    "Condition": "to comply with the Rules or to prevent a loss to SERVICERS"
  }
}
\end{minted}

\noindent
\textbf{Failure Analysis:} The true governing predicate of this clause is ``shall cease'', and the true Agent is ``ISO''. However, the model anchored onto the subordinate purposive clause at the end of the sentence (``to comply with the Rules or to prevent a loss''). Because the QA model calculates probabilities in isolation for each role, it aggressively assigned this subordinate phrase as the Predicate, Agent, and Condition. This highlights a fundamental flaw in using SQuAD-trained models for legal extraction: they lack the strict hierarchical awareness of a dependency tree, allowing deeply embedded sub-clauses to erroneously overwrite the primary contractual mandate.

\medskip

\subsubsection*{Syntactic Hallucinations on Legal Boilerplate}
A secondary limitation is the model's occasional failure to distinguish between semantic actions and structural boilerplate formatting. Because the model was fine-tuned on general-domain Wikipedia articles (SQuAD 2.0), it struggles with the anomalous punctuation, redaction markers, and enumerated list markers prevalent in commercial contracts.

\medskip

\begin{quote}
  \texttt{``and (vi) To immediately advise both SERVICERS and Visa and/or MasterCard if any unauthorized person or external entity seeks access...''}
\end{quote}

\begin{minted}[fontsize=\footnotesize,breaklines,linenos]{json}
{
  "predicate": "vi",
  "roles": {
    "Theme": "Confidential Information",
    "Time": "whether by legal proceeding or otherwise"
  }
}
\end{minted}

\noindent
\textbf{Failure Analysis:} When prompted with ``What is the main action, requirement, or verb?'', the model hallucinated and extracted the Roman numeral list marker \emph{vi} as the Predicate, completely missing the actual verb ``advise''. Similarly, in redacted clauses, the model occasionally extracted \texttt{***} as the action. This indicates that while the confidence threshold (\texttt{score > 0.05}) effectively filters out null roles, it does not prevent the model from assigning high statistical confidence to structural noise.

\vspace{1em}
\subsubsection*{Proposed Mitigation Strategy}

To resolve these vulnerabilities in a production environment, a purely generative or extractive QA approach is insufficient. The optimal mitigation strategy would involve a \textbf{Hybrid Neuro-Symbolic Pipeline}. By feeding the Universal Dependency parse tree (generated in \result{Section~\ref{task_1.3}}) into the QA-SRL module, the system could enforce strict Part-of-Speech (POS) constraints. For instance, the system could algorithmically restrict the QA model's Predicate search space to tokens explicitly tagged as \texttt{ROOT} or \texttt{VERB}, physically preventing the model from extracting list markers (``vi'') or entire subordinate clauses as the main action. This hybrid approach would combine the best of neural extraction (flexibility and coverage) with hard symbolic constraints (precision and explainability), substantially improving resilience to legal domain edge cases.

\newpage\section{Clause Intent Classification}
\label{task_2.3}

This task aims to automatically categorize individual clauses extracted from a legal contract into their primary functional intent. This forms a crucial step in intelligent contract analysis, enabling downstream applications such as obligation tracking, risk assessment, termination monitoring, and compliance automation.

In this task, each clause from the Sponsorship Agreement (dated January 29, 2007, between iPayment, Inc., First Data Merchant Services Corporation, and Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.) was classified into one of seven specific categories: \textbf{Obligation}, \textbf{Prohibition}, \textbf{Condition}, \textbf{Permission}, \textbf{Representation/Warranty}, \textbf{Definition}, or \textbf{Scope/Structure}

\subsection*{Problem Statement}
While earlier pipeline stages successfully isolate individual clauses and extract key entities (such as Parties, Dates, and Monetary values), raw text remains fundamentally unstructured. A deterministic, rule-based query engine -- as required in \result{Assignment~\ref{assignment_3}} -- cannot interpret raw strings to answer complex logical queries like ``When is Party B penalized?'' or ``What actions are prohibited?'' without understanding the underlying semantic function of the text.

\medskip

Therefore, the challenge is to build a classification module that acts as a critical bridge between syntactic text extraction and intelligent retrieval. The system must not only accurately classify complex, multi-layered sentences but also resolve \emph{intent hijacking} -- where secondary conditions or permissions within a clause obscure the primary operational verb. Furthermore, to satisfy Explainable AI (XAI) requirements, the implementation must trace every classification back to a specific triggered rule, ensuring the downstream query engine is both deterministic and fully grounded in explainable logic.

\subsection{Intent Schema Definition}
To effectively transition from raw syntactic extraction to semantic analysis, the classification engine requires a high-resolution schema. Generic categorization is insufficient for a legal contract, where the operational reality of an ``Agent'' and ``Predicate'' drastically changes depending on whether an action is mandatory, forbidden, or optional. To meet the requirements of the downstream Rule-Based Query System (\result{Assignment~\ref{assignment_3}}), we defined a rigid, seven-category intent schema tailored specifically to commercial contract structures:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2cm, style=nextline]
  \item[Obligation:] Clauses that dictate a mandatory action or duty an entity must perform. These are typically anchored by imperative modal verbs such as \textit{shall}, \textit{must}, \textit{agrees to}, or \textit{will}. Identifying Obligations is critical for querying what actions a party is legally bound to execute.

  \item[Prohibition:] Clauses that dictate negative constraints or forbidden actions. These are anchored by restrictive phrasing such as \textit{shall not}, \textit{may not}, or \textit{in no event shall}. Prohibitions must be strictly isolated from Obligations to prevent the query engine from accidentally retrieving a forbidden action as a required duty.

  \item[Condition:] Clauses that establish logical prerequisites, situational triggers, or exceptions. Anchored by phrases like \textit{if}, \textit{provided that}, or \textit{in the event}, these clauses alert the downstream system that an associated Obligation or Permission is not absolute, but contingent on a specific state.

  \item[Permission:] Clauses that grant discretionary rights, powers, or authorizations without enforcing a duty. These are identified by permissive language such as \textit{may}, \textit{entitled to}, or \textit{reserves the right}.

  \item[Representation/Warranty:] Clauses that establish agreed-upon statements of fact, legal acknowledgments, or disclaimers between the parties (e.g., \textit{represents}, \textit{warrants}, \textit{acknowledges}).

  \item[Definition:] Clauses strictly dedicated to terminological scoping. Anchored by phrases like \textit{shall mean}, \textit{referred to as}, or \textit{is defined as}, this category provides the grounded dictionary that the query engine uses to resolve specific domain terms (e.g., ``What defines the Merchant Portfolio?'').

  \item[Scope/Structure:] A structural catch-all used to classify document organization, such as Exhibit headers, Section titles, and schedule declarations. Filtering these out ensures that boilerplate formatting does not pollute the operational logic of the query engine.
\end{description}

By mapping the raw text to this specific schema, the unstructured document is converted into a deterministic matrix. This allows the Rule-Based Query Engine to parse not just who is doing what, but the strict legal nature of that action.

\subsection{Methodology: Hybrid Rule-Based Classification}
To operationalize the intent schema described above, we developed a \textbf{hybrid rule-based classification engine} in Python. Rather than relying on probabilistic machine learning models -- which can introduce unpredictable hallucinations -- a deterministic approach was selected to ensure the downstream Rule-Based Query System (\result{Assignment~\ref{assignment_3}}) receives 100\% predictable and grounded data. The methodology relies on three core technical pillars:

\subsubsection*{1. Linguistic Filters}
The foundation of the classifier is a dictionary of highly specific Regular Expressions (Regex). Each intent category is mapped to a set of linguistic triggers commonly found in commercial contracts. For example, the \texttt{prohib\_keyword} rule scans for exact boundary matches such as \verb|\b(shall not|may not|must not)\b|, while the \texttt{cond\_keyword} rule targets \verb|\b(if|provided that|in the event)\b|. This allows the engine to robustly parse semantic intent without requiring complex syntactic dependency trees.

\subsubsection*{2. Classification Hierarchy}
Legal language is often layered, with modifiers capable of inverting meaning. To prevent misclassification, the engine enforces a strict evaluation hierarchy. Critically, \textbf{Prohibitions} are always evaluated before \textbf{Obligations}. For example, if a clause contains ``shall not," it is immediately and irrevocably classified as a Prohibition -- no other rule is permitted to override it, even if ``shall" (an Obligation trigger) is also present. This ensures that forbidden actions are not wrongly recorded as required duties.

\subsubsection*{3. The ``Earliest Index'' Algorithm}
Initial testing revealed a critical edge case known as ``intent hijacking": many complex clauses contain more than one operational verb. For example, a clause may dictate an obligation but conclude with a discretionary permission (e.g., ``ISO shall cease its use... as may be required''). If a standard rule loop were used, a Permission keyword (``may'') encountered later in the sentence could inadvertently overwrite the primary Obligation detected earlier.

\medskip

To solve this, we implemented an \textbf{Earliest Index} algorithm. Instead of merely returning the first rule to match in code order, the classifier evaluates \emph{all} rules, records the character index at which each match is found, and then assigns the final classification based on the keyword appearing \emph{earliest} in the clause:

\begin{minted}[fontsize=\footnotesize,breaklines,linenos]{python}
best_match = ("Other", "no_match")
earliest_index = float('inf')

for pattern, intent, rule_name in rules:
  match = re.search(pattern, text_lower)
  if match:
    # Update classification only if this keyword appears earlier in the sentence
    if match.start() < earliest_index:
      earliest_index = match.start()
      best_match = (intent, rule_name)
\end{minted}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
  \footnotesize
  \caption{Clause Intent Classification via Earliest-Index Matching}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Procedure{ClassifyClauses}{$C, \mathcal{R}$} \Comment{$C$: Input clauses, $\mathcal{R}$: Ordered rule tuples $\langle pattern, intent, trigger \rangle$}
    \State $results \gets [\ ]$
    \For{\textbf{each} $clause$ \textbf{in} $C$}
    \If{\Call{IsStructuralScope}{$clause$}}
    \State \Call{Append}{$results, \langle clause, \text{"Scope/Structure"}, \text{"struct\_keyword"} \rangle$}
    \State \textbf{continue}
    \EndIf

    \State $best\_intent \gets \text{"Other"}$
    \State $best\_trigger \gets \text{"no\_match"}$
    \State $min\_index \gets \infty$

    \For{\textbf{each} $\langle pattern, intent, trigger \rangle$ \textbf{in} $\mathcal{R}$}
    \State $match\_index \gets$ \Call{FindFirstMatch}{$pattern, clause$}

    \If{$match\_index \neq \textbf{null}$ \textbf{and} $match\_index < min\_index$}
    \State $min\_index \gets match\_index$
    \State $best\_intent \gets intent$
    \State $best\_trigger \gets trigger$
    \EndIf
    \EndFor

    \State \Call{Append}{$results, \langle clause, best\_intent, best\_trigger \rangle$}
    \EndFor

    \State \Return \Call{SerializeToJSON}{$results$}
    \EndProcedure
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Because the primary legal intent in a clause is almost universally established by its \emph{first} operational verb, this index-based matching ensures that trailing permissions or secondary conditions do not override the core classification. This simple and robust technique proved highly effective, stabilizing the classifier output and guaranteeing that the data piped into \result{Assignment~\ref{assignment_3}} is both deterministic and highly accurate.

\subsection{Technical Evaluation}
The hybrid classification engine successfully processed the raw clauses from the Sponsorship Agreement. By applying the \texttt{earliest\_index} algorithm and hierarchical linguistic filters, the system bypassed the ``intent hijacking'' problem and mapped every unstructured text string to a deterministic legal intent.

\subsubsection*{Output Data Structure (JSON).}
To serve as the foundational database for the downstream Rule-Based Query Engine (Task 3.2), the results were serialized into a structured \emph{JSON} format. Each parsed clause was mapped to an object containing four critical key-value pairs:
\begin{itemize}[topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt, itemsep=0pt]
  \item \textbf{clause\_id}: The integer index maintaining document order.
  \item \textbf{clause}: The raw text string.
  \item \textbf{intent}: The resolved operational category (e.g., Obligation, Prohibition, Condition).
  \item \textbf{trigger\_rule}: The specific Explainable AI (XAI) metadata indicating exactly which linguistic rule forced the classification.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Challenges and Limitations}
\subsubsection*{The ``Split Definition'' Edge Case}
While the \texttt{earliest\_index} algorithm successfully resolved the ``intent hijacking'' problem for operational clauses, it inadvertently created a new edge case regarding \textbf{Definitions}. In legal drafting, definitions are frequently structured using split phrases containing operational verbs, such as ``FDMS and Bank will collectively be referred to as...'' or ``Minimum Balance shall have the meaning provided in...''. Because words like \emph{``will''} and \emph{``shall''} appeared physically earlier in the string than the definitional keywords (``referred to as'', ``meaning provided in''), the \texttt{earliest\_index} algorithm initially misclassified these definitional clauses as Obligations.
\begin{minted}[fontsize=\footnotesize,breaklines,linenos]{json}
{
  "clause_id": 62,
  "clause": "\"Minimum Balance\" shall have the meaning provided in Section 6.1(a).",
  "intent": "Definition",
  "trigger_rule": "def_keyword"
}
\end{minted}

Initially, the \texttt{earliest\_index} algorithm classified Clause 62 as an Obligation because it encountered the word 'shall' at index 18, well before it reached the definitional phrase 'meaning provided in' at index 33. This necessitated an update to the regex engine to treat shall.*?meaning provided in as a single, combined definition trigger to successfully override the generic obligation rule.

\medskip

To resolve this, the regular expression for the \texttt{def\_keyword} rule had to be expanded to capture the entire split phrase (e.g., \texttt{will.*?be referred to as}). This allowed the engine to recognize the full definitional structure at the index of the first operational verb, successfully overriding the generic Obligation rule.

\subsubsection*{Limitations of a Pure Rule-Based Approach}
The primary limitation of this implementation is the inherent rigidity of rule-based linguistic filtering. Unlike the Transformer-based approach used in \result{Section~\ref{task_2.1}} (NER), a regular expression engine lacks deep semantic understanding. It relies entirely on the presence of exact string matches.

\begin{minted}[fontsize=\footnotesize,breaklines,linenos]{json}
  {
    "clause_id": 579,
    "clause": "ISO is not authorized hereunder to hold itself out as an agent of SERVICERS or to inform or represent...",
    "intent": "Other",
    "trigger_rule": "no_match"
  }
\end{minted}

Clause 579 exposes the core trade-off inherent in dictionary-based, rule-driven intent classification. In this example, the phrase \emph{``is not authorized''} semantically marks a clear \textbf{Prohibition} -- ISO is strictly forbidden to represent itself as a SERVICERS agent. However, the implemented \texttt{prohib\_keyword} rule only detects canonical boilerplate patterns such as ``shall not,'' ``may not,'' or ``is prohibited from.'' Because ``is not authorized'' was not present in the rule dictionary, the clause defaulted to the ``Other'' class, despite its clear legal meaning.

\medskip

Legal language is notoriously variable and context-dependent; relying on a fixed set of surface patterns leaves the system vulnerable to missed or misclassified intents when new linguistic forms arise. In practice, contract drafters may employ diverse phrasings (e.g., ``is not empowered to'', ``does not have the right to'', ``is precluded from'') to express prohibitions, permissions, or obligations. Without manual expansion to cover these variants, the system's recall for non-boilerplate patterns will remain limited.

\medskip

Consequently, if a novel contract utilizes highly unusual phrasing to dictate an obligation (e.g., ``ISO assumes the burden of\ldots''), and that specific phrase is absent from the rule dictionary, the engine will fail to classify it and default to the ``Other'' category.

\subsubsection*{Justification for the Trade-off}
Despite its brittleness compared to probabilistic machine learning models, the deterministic rule-based approach was intentionally selected to satisfy the strict requirements of \result{Assignment~\ref{assignment_3}}. This choice A Rule-Based Query System requires 100\% predictable, explainable data. By accepting the limitation of a rigid dictionary, the system entirely eliminates the risk of ``semantic hallucinations'' (where a model might incorrectly guess the intent of a clause). Furthermore, the deterministic nature of this implementation ensures that every classification is accompanied by a transparent \texttt{trigger\_rule}, fully satisfying the Explainable AI (XAI) mandate of the final deliverable.
